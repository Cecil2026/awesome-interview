# Universal Interview Checklist

Use this regardless of which roadmap you followed. The checklist starts two weeks before your first onsite and ends with a post-interview reflection. Print it, copy it to Notion, or fork the repo and tick boxes in GitHub — whichever keeps you accountable.

The pattern: ramp down, not up. Most candidates cram in the final days and arrive exhausted. The candidates who get offers taper.

## 2 weeks before

The last window where new work helps. Use it for logistics and final mocks, not new material.

- [ ] Confirm the interview format with your recruiter in writing: number of rounds, length of each, coding tool used (CoderPad, HackerRank, CodeSignal, shared doc, whiteboard), allowed languages, what each round covers
- [ ] Confirm the names and roles of your interviewers — research each one on LinkedIn so you can ask informed questions
- [ ] Schedule 2-3 mock interviews (peer, paid service like interviewing.io or Pramp, or a trusted senior engineer)
- [ ] Identify your two weakest topic areas from your prep and schedule extra drill sessions on them this week only
- [ ] Draft your 5 strongest STAR stories and ensure each maps to at least 2 of: leadership, conflict, ambiguity, failure, technical depth, cross-team collaboration, customer impact
- [ ] Write down 5-7 questions to ask interviewers, grouped by interviewer type (peer engineer, hiring manager, skip-level, recruiter). Make them specific to this team, not generic.
- [ ] Confirm logistics: time zone of the interview, calendar invites accepted, video conferencing tool installed and tested
- [ ] If on-site: confirm address, parking or transit, dress code, who you're meeting at reception, photo ID requirements
- [ ] If remote: test camera, mic, and screen share with a friend on the actual platform (Zoom, Meet, Teams)

## 1 week before

Light review only. No new material. Sleep well.

- [ ] Re-read your own notes from your roadmap — do not start any new chapter or course
- [ ] Re-solve 1 problem per day from the categories you found hardest. 25 minutes max. Stop after.
- [ ] Re-read your STAR stories daily, out loud, once each
- [ ] One final mock if you can get it — full loop or single round, your choice
- [ ] Pick your interview outfit (even for remote — looking sharp changes your posture and tone). Iron it. Hang it up.
- [ ] Confirm again that your video setup works: lighting on your face, mic clear, background uncluttered, second monitor positioned so your eyes stay near the camera
- [ ] If using a separate whiteboard or scratch tool (Excalidraw, OneNote, iPad), open it and verify it works the way you expect
- [ ] Plan your meals for interview day — easy to digest, nothing new, nothing heavy
- [ ] Cut caffeine intake to your normal baseline if you've been over-indexing during prep
- [ ] Start sleeping 8 hours every night this week. The night before is too late to start.

## Day before

Stop studying after lunch. Your brain consolidates overnight, not at 11pm.

- [ ] Light review of STAR stories in the morning only (30 minutes max)
- [ ] Re-read the job description and the team description so context is fresh
- [ ] Re-read 3 of your prepared questions for each interviewer type
- [ ] Lay out your outfit
- [ ] Lay out your interview-day kit: water bottle, light snack (banana, granola bar — nothing crumbly or messy), notepad, two working pens, charger, headphones if remote
- [ ] If remote: confirm the calendar invite has the correct link, that the link works, and that you can join 5 minutes early
- [ ] If on-site: pack your bag with ID, water, snack, notepad, pens, charger, and any required paperwork
- [ ] No technical study after 6pm. Do something genuinely relaxing — walk, dinner with someone you like, a movie. Not a hard movie.
- [ ] No alcohol
- [ ] Phone off, in another room, lights out by 10pm. Aim for 8 hours of sleep.

## Day of

The plan: arrive calm, leave on time, no surprises.

**Morning**
- [ ] Wake up at least 2 hours before the first interview
- [ ] Normal breakfast. Eat something with protein. Hydrate.
- [ ] Light stretch or short walk. Sit still for 20 minutes if that's your style — whatever puts you in your body.
- [ ] Do 3 minutes of box breathing (4 seconds in, 4 hold, 4 out, 4 hold) before starting

**Pre-interview (30 minutes before)**
- [ ] Restroom
- [ ] Water bottle filled and within reach
- [ ] Snack within reach
- [ ] Notepad open, pens ready
- [ ] STAR story summary card in your peripheral vision (one line per story — situation, task, action, result)
- [ ] List of questions for this interviewer visible
- [ ] If remote: join the call 5 minutes early, camera and mic on, smile when the interviewer joins
- [ ] If on-site: arrive at the building 10 minutes before reception time

**During each interview**
- [ ] Confirm interviewer's name and role at the start
- [ ] For coding rounds: clarify the problem out loud before writing any code. Restate the input, output, and constraints. Ask about edge cases.
- [ ] For system design: clarify scope first — functional requirements, non-functional requirements, scale estimates. Do not jump to architecture.
- [ ] For behavioral: pick one story, deliver STAR in 90-120 seconds, then pause for follow-ups. Resist the urge to keep talking.
- [ ] Think out loud. Silence reads as "stuck" to interviewers even when you're making progress.
- [ ] If you don't know something, say so, then propose how you'd find out. Never bluff.
- [ ] Save 3-5 minutes at the end for your questions. Have them ready.

**Between interviews**
- [ ] Restroom every break
- [ ] Drink water every break
- [ ] Bite of snack if there's a long block
- [ ] Do not replay the previous round in your head. It's done. The next round is the only one you can affect.
- [ ] 3 box breaths between rounds

**After the loop**
- [ ] Walk outside if you can
- [ ] Eat a real meal
- [ ] Within 24 hours: send a brief thank-you email to your recruiter, and (if appropriate for the company culture) to each interviewer. Two sentences. No follow-up questions in this email.

## Post-interview reflection

Do this within 24 hours, while memory is fresh. Future-you needs this data.

Copy this template into a private doc and fill it in for every loop you do.

## If the interview went badly

Some interviews go badly. This is expected, not catastrophic.

- [ ] Do the reflection anyway. Especially this one. The data is more valuable when it hurt.
- [ ] Identify whether it was a knowledge gap, a communication gap, a nerves gap, or a fit gap — different problems have different fixes
- [ ] Do not spiral. One bad loop is signal about one moment, not about you.
- [ ] Schedule the next mock within a week. Forward motion is the antidote.
- [ ] If you have other loops scheduled within the next week, do not over-correct. Trust your prep. Show up.

## If the interview went well

- [ ] Still do the reflection. Wins teach as much as losses, but only if you write them down.
- [ ] Note exactly what worked — phrasing, framing, story choice. Reuse it.
- [ ] Do not coast. The offer is not signed until it is signed.
- [ ] If you get an offer, do not respond immediately with a yes. Take at least 24 hours. Negotiate.

## Final reminder

Interviews are a skill. You can be great at the job and bad at the interview, and you can be great at the interview and unprepared for the job. This checklist tunes the first variable. Your roadmap and your career tune the second one.

Good luck.
