# Amazon Leadership Principles

Amazon's 16 Leadership Principles (LPs) are the explicit rubric for every Amazon interview loop. A "bar raiser" — an interviewer trained to assess LP fit independent of the team's hiring need — scores every candidate against the principles. The same framework is widely copied (or echoed) at AWS, Audible, Twitch, Whole Foods, and increasingly outside Amazon.

The practical implication: every story you bring should be tagged with the 1-2 principles it most strongly demonstrates. Prepare 10-14 stories that collectively cover all 16 principles. Be ready for follow-up drilldown questions ("what would you do differently?", "what was your specific contribution?", "what was the data?").

Use the STAR structure (see `README.md`). For each principle below, this file describes what Amazon actually means by it, what questions probe it, and how to prep a story.

---

## 1. Customer Obsession

**Amazon's description:** Leaders start with the customer and work backwards. They work vigorously to earn and keep customer trust. Although leaders pay attention to competitors, they obsess over customers.

**What it really means in interviews:**
You can name the customer (internal or external), articulate their pain in their words, and show that your decisions traced back to that pain. Working back from competitors or revenue targets does not count. The interviewer is also watching for whether you sacrificed something (time, scope, your own preference) for the customer.

**Common questions probing this principle:**
- Tell me about a time you went above and beyond for a customer.
- Describe a time you used customer feedback to change a product or process.
- Tell me about a decision where you put customer needs above business or team interests.

**How to prep your STAR story:**
- Pick a situation where the customer's voice was not the obvious priority at the start — you brought it in.
- Action: name the customer research, ticket review, or call you did personally. Show you went to the source.
- Result: a customer-visible improvement plus an upstream change (process, policy) so future customers benefit too.

**Anti-pattern (avoid):**
- "We talked to a few customers and built what they asked for" — sounds like order-taking, not obsession.
- Citing internal stakeholders as customers without acknowledging that distinction.

## 2. Ownership

**Amazon's description:** Leaders are owners. They think long term and don't sacrifice long-term value for short-term results. They act on behalf of the entire company, beyond just their own team. They never say "that's not my job."

**What it really means in interviews:**
You take responsibility past the boundary of your job description, and you weigh second- and third-order effects on the broader business. Interviewers probe especially for moments where you fixed something that was not in your lane and for moments where you resisted a quick win that would have created downstream cost.

**Common questions probing this principle:**
- Tell me about a time you took on something outside your scope.
- Describe a time you sacrificed a short-term win for a long-term outcome.
- Tell me about a time you owned a failure that involved multiple teams.

**How to prep your STAR story:**
- Situation where the "right" path was harder and longer than the easy path.
- Action: explicitly name the short-term win you turned down and why. Name where you crossed team boundaries.
- Result: durable outcome that paid back over multiple quarters.

**Anti-pattern (avoid):**
- A "long-term" story that was actually only a few weeks.
- Taking ownership in name only — you raised the issue but did not actually drive resolution.

## 3. Invent and Simplify

**Amazon's description:** Leaders expect and require innovation and invention from their teams and always find ways to simplify. They are externally aware, look for new ideas from everywhere, and are not limited by "not invented here." As we do new things, we accept that we may be misunderstood for long periods of time.

**What it really means in interviews:**
Two halves: invented something genuinely new, *or* meaningfully simplified something accidentally complex. Bonus for borrowing an idea from outside your team/industry and adapting it. Interviewers are skeptical of "novel" claims, so be specific about prior art and what you actually added.

**Common questions probing this principle:**
- Tell me about a time you invented a new solution or process.
- Describe a time you simplified a complex system.
- Tell me about a time an idea of yours was misunderstood at first.

**How to prep your STAR story:**
- Pick something with a clear "before" complexity or absence.
- Action: name the standard approach and why you rejected it. Show the prior art you drew on.
- Result: simplification quantified in steps removed, lines deleted, time saved; or new capability adopted by others.

**Anti-pattern (avoid):**
- A "new" idea that is standard industry practice you just had not used before.
- A rewrite that did not actually reduce complexity (only moved it).

## 4. Are Right, A Lot

**Amazon's description:** Leaders are right a lot. They have strong judgment and good instincts. They seek diverse perspectives and work to disconfirm their beliefs.

**What it really means in interviews:**
This is *not* about always being right. It is about having a track record of good judgment under uncertainty *and* actively seeking out perspectives that might prove you wrong. Show your work: how do you make calls, and how do you check yourself?

**Common questions probing this principle:**
- Tell me about a time you had to make a call with incomplete information.
- Describe a time your initial instinct turned out to be wrong.
- Tell me about a decision where you actively sought a dissenting view.

**How to prep your STAR story:**
- Situation with real ambiguity — no obvious right answer.
- Action: triangulation, the dissenting voice you specifically sought out, the pre-mortem or assumption list.
- Result: outcome, and your honest accounting of which assumptions held vs. failed.

**Anti-pattern (avoid):**
- A story that just demonstrates you were right (lacks the "how you check yourself" part).
- Picking a slam-dunk decision with no ambiguity.

## 5. Learn and Be Curious

**Amazon's description:** Leaders are never done learning and always seek to improve themselves. They are curious about new possibilities and act to explore them.

**What it really means in interviews:**
Concrete, recent learning — and not just because you had to. Bonus if the learning was outside your formal role and led somewhere. Interviewers want self-directed depth, not "I took a class my manager paid for."

**Common questions probing this principle:**
- Tell me about something significant you have learned recently outside your day job.
- Describe a time your curiosity led you to discover something important.
- Tell me about a domain you taught yourself for a project.

**How to prep your STAR story:**
- Self-initiated learning, recent (last 1-2 years).
- Action: what you did concretely — books, source code, mentors, prototypes. The hours invested.
- Result: applied the learning to ship something or to teach others.

**Anti-pattern (avoid):**
- Generic "I love learning" with no specific recent example.
- Learning that never connected to action.

## 6. Hire and Develop the Best

**Amazon's description:** Leaders raise the performance bar with every hire and promotion. They recognize exceptional talent, and willingly move them throughout the organization. Leaders develop leaders and take seriously their role in coaching others. We work on behalf of our people to invent mechanisms for development like Career Choice.

**What it really means in interviews:**
For ICs: how you have mentored, raised the bar in hiring loops, given feedback, advocated for others. For managers: hiring decisions, performance management, growing people to the next level. Concrete examples of *named individuals* whose trajectory you affected.

**Common questions probing this principle:**
- Tell me about someone you mentored or developed.
- Describe a time you raised the bar in a hiring decision.
- Tell me about feedback you gave that changed someone's trajectory.

**How to prep your STAR story:**
- A specific person, a specific intervention, a specific outcome (promotion, role change, capability gained).
- Action: what you did to coach, advocate, or challenge.
- Result: their trajectory, ideally with a moment they acknowledged your role.

**Anti-pattern (avoid):**
- Vague "I mentor a lot of people" with no specific story.
- Taking credit for someone's growth that was mostly their own work.

## 7. Insist on the Highest Standards

**Amazon's description:** Leaders have relentlessly high standards — many people may think these standards are unreasonably high. Leaders are continually raising the bar and drive their teams to deliver high quality products, services, and processes. Leaders ensure that defects do not get sent down the line and that problems are fixed so they stay fixed.

**What it really means in interviews:**
You held a line on quality when it would have been easier not to. You raised standards across a team or system. Defects do not just get patched — they get root-caused and prevented. Watch for the trade-off conversation: high standards should not paralyze, so show how you balanced speed.

**Common questions probing this principle:**
- Tell me about a time you raised the quality bar on a team.
- Describe a time you refused to ship something that was not ready.
- Tell me about a recurring defect you eliminated.

**How to prep your STAR story:**
- A specific defect, gap, or low standard you would not accept.
- Action: what you put in place — review process, automation, definition of done. How you held the line without becoming a blocker.
- Result: defects prevented, or quality metric improved, sustained over time.

**Anti-pattern (avoid):**
- Perfectionism that delayed everything (interviewer reads this as not pragmatic).
- A one-time fix with no systemic change.

## 8. Think Big

**Amazon's description:** Thinking small is a self-fulfilling prophecy. Leaders create and communicate a bold direction that inspires results. They think differently and look around corners for ways to serve customers.

**What it really means in interviews:**
You can articulate a vision that goes beyond the immediate ticket or quarter. You convinced others of it. The story usually involves either a substantial scope expansion or reframing a small problem as part of a much bigger opportunity.

**Common questions probing this principle:**
- Tell me about a time you set an ambitious goal others thought was unrealistic.
- Describe a time you reframed a small problem as a much bigger opportunity.
- Tell me about a vision you communicated that mobilized people.

**How to prep your STAR story:**
- An initial scope or framing that you challenged as too small.
- Action: how you constructed the bigger vision (market sizing, customer interviews, future scenario). How you sold it.
- Result: actual progress against the bigger vision — not just a slide deck.

**Anti-pattern (avoid):**
- A "bold vision" that never shipped or saw progress.
- Big talk without the substantiation of how you would get there.

## 9. Bias for Action

**Amazon's description:** Speed matters in business. Many decisions and actions are reversible and do not need extensive study. We value calculated risk taking.

**What it really means in interviews:**
You moved fast and made a call rather than wait for perfect information. Crucially, you distinguished reversible from irreversible decisions — bias for action does not mean recklessness on one-way doors. Show the framework.

**Common questions probing this principle:**
- Tell me about a time you moved quickly without all the information.
- Describe a calculated risk you took.
- Tell me about a time waiting would have been the wrong call.

**How to prep your STAR story:**
- A reversible decision where speed mattered.
- Action: how fast you moved, what you did to make it cheaply reversible, the explicit decision to not wait.
- Result: speed advantage gained, plus what you would have lost by waiting.

**Anti-pattern (avoid):**
- Speed on an irreversible decision (sounds reckless).
- "Acted fast" with no signal that you considered the trade-off.

## 10. Frugality

**Amazon's description:** Accomplish more with less. Constraints breed resourcefulness, self-sufficiency, and invention. There are no extra points for growing headcount, budget size, or fixed expense.

**What it really means in interviews:**
You delivered with constrained resources — and the constraint actually shaped a better solution. Not the same as cheapness; it is about resourcefulness. Show what you cut and what you got for free as a result (simpler design, faster iteration).

**Common questions probing this principle:**
- Tell me about a time you delivered under tight resource constraints.
- Describe a time you found a way to accomplish something with significantly less.
- Tell me about a time you saved meaningful cost without sacrificing outcome.

**How to prep your STAR story:**
- A real resource constraint — headcount, budget, time, or compute.
- Action: the cuts you made, the cheap substitute you found, the manual workaround that scaled enough.
- Result: cost saved + outcome preserved, ideally with a metric.

**Anti-pattern (avoid):**
- "Frugality" that resulted in tech debt or customer harm.
- A story where you simply did more with the same resources (that is not frugality, that is productivity).

## 11. Earn Trust

**Amazon's description:** Leaders listen attentively, speak candidly, and treat others respectfully. They are vocally self-critical, even when doing so is awkward or embarrassing. Leaders do not believe their or their team's body odor smells of perfume. They benchmark themselves and their teams against the best.

**What it really means in interviews:**
You built trust deliberately, through specific behaviors: candid speech, public self-criticism, owning mistakes, listening without interrupting, following through on commitments. The "body odor" line is Amazon's somewhat blunt way of saying: leaders who think their work is great without external benchmark do not earn trust.

**Common questions probing this principle:**
- Tell me about a time you earned the trust of a skeptical stakeholder.
- Describe a time you were publicly self-critical.
- Tell me about a time you rebuilt trust after damaging it.

**How to prep your STAR story:**
- A relationship that started skeptical or strained.
- Action: specific trust-building behaviors — kept a commitment, owned a mistake in writing, sought their input first, etc.
- Result: a working relationship with concrete evidence (they later advocated for you, brought you into something, gave you the benefit of the doubt under pressure).

**Anti-pattern (avoid):**
- Trust narratives that rely on "we just got along."
- No moment of candid self-criticism — central to this LP.

## 12. Dive Deep

**Amazon's description:** Leaders operate at all levels, stay connected to the details, audit frequently, and are skeptical when metrics and anecdote differ. No task is beneath them.

**What it really means in interviews:**
You went past the surface to a root cause others had missed. You read the code, the dashboard, the SQL, the support tickets — not just the summary. Bonus if you found a discrepancy between the dashboard and reality.

**Common questions probing this principle:**
- Tell me about a time you dove deep into data to find a root cause.
- Describe a time the dashboard told a different story than what was actually happening.
- Tell me about a problem you fixed that others had given up on.

**How to prep your STAR story:**
- A problem where the obvious answer was wrong.
- Action: specific tools you used (logs, SQL, profiler, user interviews), the moment you spotted the discrepancy.
- Result: root cause identified, fix that held, and a mechanism to detect similar drift earlier.

**Anti-pattern (avoid):**
- "I asked an engineer to look at it" — Dive Deep is about *you* going to the data.
- Surface-level diagnosis dressed up as deep work.

## 13. Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit

**Amazon's description:** Leaders are obligated to respectfully challenge decisions when they disagree, even when doing so is uncomfortable or exhausting. Leaders have conviction and are tenacious. They do not compromise for the sake of social cohesion. Once a decision is determined, they commit wholly.

**What it really means in interviews:**
Two-part principle. First: you disagreed when it mattered, with data and respect, even up the chain. Second: when the decision went against you, you committed visibly — not malicious compliance. Many candidates only nail one half. Hit both.

**Common questions probing this principle:**
- Tell me about a time you strongly disagreed with a decision but committed to it anyway.
- Describe a time you pushed back on a senior leader.
- Tell me about a time you held a position despite social pressure to drop it.

**How to prep your STAR story:**
- A decision with real stakes where you held a minority view.
- Action: the venue and format of your disagreement (ideally written), the moment you committed publicly, the concrete acts of support after.
- Result: outcome, plus your honest read on who was right with hindsight.

**Anti-pattern (avoid):**
- All disagreement, no commit — sounds difficult to work with.
- All commit, no disagreement — sounds like no backbone.

## 14. Deliver Results

**Amazon's description:** Leaders focus on the key inputs for their business and deliver them with the right quality and in a timely fashion. Despite setbacks, they rise to the occasion and never settle.

**What it really means in interviews:**
You shipped, on time, at quality, despite obstacles. Quantified results. Repeated pattern (not just one lucky launch). Interviewers will probe for the *specific* result — vague impact claims fail this principle hard.

**Common questions probing this principle:**
- Tell me about your most significant delivery in the last year.
- Describe a time you delivered results despite major obstacles.
- Tell me about a launch you owned end-to-end and the impact it had.

**How to prep your STAR story:**
- A clear deliverable with a clear metric.
- Action: specific obstacles and how you adjusted — re-scoping, re-staffing, escalation.
- Result: quantified, ideally with both launch and sustained-impact metrics.

**Anti-pattern (avoid):**
- "Drove significant impact" with no number.
- Heroics narrative — Deliver Results is about consistent shipping, not last-minute saves.

## 15. Strive to be Earth's Best Employer

**Amazon's description:** Leaders work every day to create a safer, more productive, higher performing, more diverse, and more just work environment. They lead with empathy, have fun at work, and make it easy for others to have fun. Leaders ask themselves: Are my fellow employees growing? Are they empowered? Are they ready for what's next? Leaders have a vision for and commitment to their employees' personal success, whether that be at Amazon or elsewhere.

**What it really means in interviews:**
You made the working environment better — for safety, inclusion, growth, or psychological safety. Concrete actions, not values statements. Bonus if you supported someone's growth even when it meant they would leave your team or the company.

**Common questions probing this principle:**
- Tell me about a time you improved the working environment for your team.
- Describe a time you supported a teammate's growth even at a cost to your project.
- Tell me about a time you advocated for inclusion or psychological safety.

**How to prep your STAR story:**
- A specific gap in the work environment you noticed.
- Action: what you did — process change, advocacy, mentorship, structural change. Show personal investment of time.
- Result: measurable improvement (retention, eNPS, promotion outcomes, named individual's trajectory).

**Anti-pattern (avoid):**
- Generic culture statements ("I value diversity") with no action.
- Performative actions with no measurable team-level effect.

## 16. Success and Scale Bring Broad Responsibility

**Amazon's description:** We started in a garage, but we're not there anymore. We are big, we impact the world, and we are far from perfect. We must be humble and thoughtful about even the secondary effects of our actions. Our local communities, planet, and future generations need us to be better every day. We must begin each day with a determination to make better, do better, and be better for our customers, our employees, our partners, and the world at large. And we must end every day knowing we can do even more tomorrow. Leaders create more than they consume and always leave things better than how they found them.

**What it really means in interviews:**
You thought about second-order effects — on users not in the target segment, on communities, on the environment, on competitors and partners, on long-term trust. You acted on that broader responsibility even when no one was making you. This is the newest LP and interviewers often probe it loosely; concrete examples of considering downstream impact win.

**Common questions probing this principle:**
- Tell me about a time you considered the broader impact of a decision beyond your immediate users.
- Describe a time you raised an ethical or societal concern at work.
- Tell me about a time you chose a path that was harder but better for stakeholders outside your team.

**How to prep your STAR story:**
- A decision where the broader-impact view was not the default — you brought it in.
- Action: how you mapped the broader stakeholders, the concrete change you advocated for, the cost you accepted.
- Result: harm avoided or benefit created, plus an institutional change (policy, review process) so the broader view is considered by default.

**Anti-pattern (avoid):**
- Vague values statements with no decision attached.
- A story that conflates "good for our brand" with broader responsibility — they sometimes align, but the LP is about cases where they do not.
