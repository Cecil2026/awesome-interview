# Behavioral Interview Prep

Behavioral interviews probe how you have actually behaved in past situations as a proxy for how you will behave on the job. Hiring managers care less about the facts of the story and more about the decisions you made, the trade-offs you weighed, and the lessons you took away.

## The STAR framework

STAR is the standard structure for answering a behavioral question. Keep each section tight; the full answer should run roughly 2-4 minutes.

- **Situation (15-20%)** — Set the context. Where were you, what was the team, what was the stakes? Two or three sentences. Avoid org-chart deep dives.
- **Task (10-15%)** — State your specific responsibility. The interviewer needs to know what *you* owned, not what the team owned.
- **Action (50-60%)** — The meat of the answer. Walk through what you did, in first person ("I", not "we"). Explain *why* you chose each step. If you collaborated, name the trade-off you made on the collaboration itself.
- **Result (15-20%)** — Quantify the outcome. Revenue, latency, headcount, retention, NPS, time saved, defects avoided. If hard numbers are not available, give a concrete qualitative signal (promotion, adoption by another team, leadership feedback).

Strong answers also include a brief *Reflection* — one sentence on what you would do differently or what the experience taught you. This signals self-awareness, which most rubrics weight heavily.

## How to use this section

1. Read `star-questions.md` and pick 8-12 stories from your own background that collectively cover the eight themes. Most real interview loops can be answered by recombining a small set of well-prepared stories.
2. Draft each story in STAR form. Write it out — do not just outline it. The act of writing surfaces vague claims.
3. Practice out loud against a timer. Aim for 3 minutes per story; cut filler.
4. If you are interviewing at Amazon (or any company that has copied their bar-raiser model), work through `amazon-leadership-principles.md` and tag each of your stories with the 2-3 principles it best demonstrates.
5. Prepare 2-3 *failure* stories. Every senior loop probes for them, and candidates who only bring success stories read as lacking self-awareness.

## Common interviewer signals

| Signal | What it means | How to surface it |
|---|---|---|
| Scope | How big was the blast radius of your decision? | Mention team size, user count, revenue at risk |
| Ownership | Did you drive, or did you ride along? | Use "I decided", "I escalated", "I rewrote" |
| Self-awareness | Do you know what you got wrong? | Volunteer the trade-off you regret |
| Judgment | Did you weigh the alternatives? | Briefly name the option you rejected and why |
